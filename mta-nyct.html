MTA New York City Transit

Native iOS/Android app for the MTA New York City Transit. In 2014, the MTA New York City Transit had a daily ridership of 5.6 million and an annual ridership of roughly 1.751 billion. The MetroCard, the payment method for the transit system, has been in use since 1993. MetroCard Vending Machines have been in use since 1999.

Overview:

My team of UX Designers and I came up with a way of assisting MTA New York City Transit commuters in refilling their MetroCard balances online, anywhere and at any time, at their convenience, and give them a way to manage multiple MetroCards.

Contextual Inquiry

To get a sense of the MTA brand and the company’s background, we conducted a contextual inquiry. This included research into the MTA’s history as a company, but also the MetroCard’s history, as that gave us more data that assisted us in our project. For example, as the MetroCard has been in use since 1994 (with Vending Machines in use since 1999), we can surmise that the current card purchasing system is an outdated one; this system was designed in a time before people had quick access to online purchasing.

Heuristic Evaluations

My fellow UX Designers and I proceeded to complete Heuristic Evaluations of these systems’ apps/sites. In other words, we evaluated them using a set of rules of thumb (Abby Covert’s Heuristics) with regards to ease in navigating and completing a task on these apps and sites.

Competitive Analysis

Though the MTA New York City Transit does not have any true competitors, we decided to look into the apps and websites of comparable transit systems in other cities–apps/sites that do allow users to refill and manage their transit passes. The transit passes that we chose included the NJ Transit pass, Boston’s CharlieCard, Washington D.C.’s smarTrip, Shenzhen’s Tong, and San Francisco’s ClipperCard.

With these other businesses’ apps/sites’ successes and pain points in mind, we set out to create our app by emulating their strong points, and avoiding their weaknesses/complexities.

User Flows

For further analysis, our design team also completed several user and task flows for the above transit systems. Doing so gives us a better sense of what flow our own eventual app should emulate (or avoid).

An additional benefit is that it allows us to quantify the number of steps it takes a user to complete a task. With these numbers in mind, we provided ourselves with a goal, a number of steps we were targeting to meet.

Personas and Scenarios

Using the trends that emerged from our Affinity Mapping (specifically those that had variations in habits, trust, and concerns, we created three personas that represented our app’s users. These personas allow us designers to design while keeping our users in mind, making sure they were are catering to their needs and including that which pleases them (and excluding that which irritates them).

With Melinda, our primary persona, in mind, my design team and I created a scenario for Melinda, who needed a way to set up an auto-fill option for her MetroCard. This scenario begins setting the stage for us to begin design, by having us to focus on what specific task our user wishes to complete (auto-fill MetroCard), and what screens are necessary for Melinda to complete her task.

Feature Prioritization

With multiple forms of research completed, we now had a sense of what kind of features MTA commuters would want in a design solution to their problem (i.e., to give them the option of MetroCard purchasing online, and on-the-go), and what kind of features comparable transit systems’ apps included.

Listing all of our features, my team of designers and I prioritized them via two methods. The first included plotting our features on a graph, with their positions depending on the impact it would have on commuters when trying to complete a tack, and also to what degree a commuter would expect a feature. Features that we would especially warrant implementation are those that have a high impact on commuters, but would also be unexpected.

The second method of prioritization was by categorizing features under four categories: Must Have Features, Should Have Features, Could Have Features, and Won’t Have Features, (MoSCoW method).

Ideation

Having prioritized features and deciding on which were central to our app, we began sketching app screens that would include these features. Through the design studio method of rapid, collaborative sketching, we produced 31 sketches in 10 minutes. After several rounds of sketching, we produced home screens and profile screens, giving users the options to add value/time to their MetroCards, access their own accounts, as well as see their transaction history and card usage history.

Google Material design Research

As the other UX Designers produced low-fidelity iOS wireframes and began testing the app on users, I began reading through Google’s Material design and researching how to design an Android version of our app.

Material provided its shares of challenges. As my design partners gave insight into how the prototype would eventually look, as they designed and iterated, it became clear to me than an Android app was not a mere translation of an iOS app.

Next Steps

There are several short and long term steps my team and I can take to further improve our MTA Kiosk app.

In the short-term, I would develop those wireframes into a working prototype to test on Android users, as mockups for an Android app are complete.

Regarding long-term steps, I would want to seek out and interview individuals of other age brackets, specifically, older individuals. Nearly 96% of our 49 initial survey respondents were between the ages of 18–40, and it would be unfair to automatically assume that any one outside of this age group would not use our app. Interviewing and taking insights from older individuals would help us cater to all MTA users.
