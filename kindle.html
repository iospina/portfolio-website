Amazon Kindle

Designing a new social feature for Amazon Kindle's native iOS app. Many people enjoy having access to books through the Amazon Kindle app, and while the app provides a pleasant reading experience, it currently doesn’t connect them to other readers or other books they might enjoy.

Completed:

July 2016

Industry:

E-reader

Project Duties:

Competitive Analysis
Feature Prioritization
User Interviews
Affinity Mapping
Personas
Scenarios
Task Flows
Annotated Wireframes
App Mockup

Overview:

My team and I were tasked with connecting Amazon users in order to make the Kindle reading experience more social, by giving readers ways to share and discuss the books they’re reading. Doing so would increase Amazon customer engagement by introducing new opportunities for social engagement that extend and enhance the Kindle reading experience.

User Research

The three of us began our project by putting together and sending out a survey screener to get a sense of who we should interview. From the responses to these questions, we wanted to see if our potential interviewees would fall under the category of our target users (ebook readers). We also got a basic sense of what products these individuals, use and their habits surrounding these products. After receiving 15 responses to our survey, we found 9 potential interviewees, and my teammate and I proceeded to interview 7 of them.

Brand & Competitor Research

My team researched competing reading apps. As it turns out, some of our interviewees were familiar with several of these competitors, including: iBooks, nook, GoodReads, and Glose. It wasn’t enough to analyze just any reading app, however. As we were tasked with creating for Kindle a social experience around books, it was important to look at what apps combined a reading experience with a social experience. Of the four, we decided that the most effective of such social reading apps were GoodReads and Glose, and focused on them.

Data Synthesis

Having completed our User Research via interviews, my teammate and I proceeded to do an Affinity Mapping. This process included taking single insights from our interviews and writing them on post-it notes. We then took these post-it notes, put them on a white board, and then organized them by common points. Doing so allows for trends to naturally emerge.

Personas

Using these trends, we developed personas, that is, fictional users of our app. This process is key in humanizing and giving our eventual users a face, and allowing us to design with these specific personas, "Danny" and "Phillipa," in mind. The trends that would later coalesce under our primary persona “Danny” included his habits and needs as an e-book reader.

Feature Prioritization

Bringing together our research findings, the three of us listed all possible features that we could adopt from competitors, but also features that our personas would clearly need.

We began this process by listing all features, then narrowing the ones that we must focus on by priority. We achieved this in various ways: by weighing the importance of the feature to the business (Amazon), to each persona (while weighing features important to our primary persona Danny more than those important to our other persona), and ultimately labeling these features by which we Must, Should, Could, and Won’t (MoSCoW Method) focus on.

Sketching

With these must-have features in mind, the three of us began sketching wireframes through the Design Studio technique. This technique involved us sketching 6–8 sketches each, with a specific feature or screen in mind for 5 minutes. Afterwards, we would explain quickly present our sketches to each other, critique each other, and go through several other rounds of sketching all while adopting design concepts from each other and improving our own designs. After a couple of rounds, we ended with 76 sketches and began coalescing our common concepts into single design screens.

Lo-Fidelity Usability Testing

With our now single design concept in mind, I sketched out lo-fidelity wireframes, from which a prototype could be designed using the POP app, an app that allows for rapid prototyping by using photos of wireframes. We tested our app with three people and were able to gather feedback we then iterated and applied to our higher fidelity prototype

Iterations

Taking our users’ feedback into account, we iterated on our designs. We removed the “Follow Collection” buttons from users’ profile screens, and redesigned the “Leave a Note” experience.

Moving Forward

There were several features that fell into our Should & Could categories when conducting our feature prioritization, that we felt would be a solid logical direction for the app to head in.

Among these potential features there are a public, SoundCloud-inspired, "Leave A Note" sidebar. A second feature would be "ReadWithMe" emulating social reading apps such as GoodReads that allow users to track other users’ progress when reading books. A third ppossible feature would allow users to answer a short list of general questions regarding a book to avoid overly-detailed reviews that would give essentially away a book's plot
